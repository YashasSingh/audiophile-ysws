Tick Tock

Tick Tock is a program where students can learn to bridge the gap between digital logic and physical craftsmanship by designing and building their very own custom Pocket Watch or any sort of smaller physical watch. The goal is to design a custom PCB, modeling a bespoke case, and finally assembling the physical hardware into a functional timepiece that blends their own taste within their engineering.
For students, the process is streamlined through a dedicated platform that allows them to:

Access deep-dive guides on schematic capture and PCB routing specifically for small-form-factor electronics.
Download base templates for mechanical cases to ensure internal components fit perfectly while leaving room for creative exterior flair.
Submit their design files (Gerbers and STLs) directly through the portal to have their custom boards manufactured and their cases 3D printed and shipped to them.

The curriculum is designed to be accessible to hardware novices while remaining challenging for veteran makers. The project is structured into a minimum 7-hour requirement, this is subject to change after at least 1 round of testers. Multiple guides will be written down before to help the students during researching and schematic drawing.
To ensure a high-quality final product, the project is divided into focused modules:

2 Hours: Schematic Design – Mapping out the "brain" of the watch and selecting additional components, this should take a higher part of their time compared to other sections.
2 Hours: PCB Layout – If students decide to follow tutorial pocket watch, they must show difference here as well in terms of overall watch design itself
2 Hours: Case Design (CAD) – Designing a protective, aesthetic enclosure that houses the PCB and battery perfectly.
1 Hour: Miscellaneous/Assembly – Fine-tuning the BOM (Bill of Materials), documentation, and final preparation for manufacturing.
To maintain integrity and document the "maker's journey," students must follow two main rules:
The Lapse Requirement: All work must be recorded using Lapse (or similar time-lapse software). This provides a "proof of work" video that captures the design process from a blank canvas to a finished assembly.
The Maker’s Journal: Students must keep a live journal during the build with 3–5 entries. These should be short reflections (1–2 sentences) on challenges faced or breakthroughs made during specific chunks of the design.
Upon successful completion and verification of the 7-hour build and time-lapse, students will receive:
The Hardware Grant: Their custom-designed PCB manufactured and shipped + their case 3D printed in the filament of their choice.
The Component Kit: A curated set of internal parts (quartz crystal, battery, buttons, and display/hands) to bring the watch to life. (Plus grant for external time if extra time is spent)
